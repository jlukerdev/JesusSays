# CLASSIFICATION_RULES.md
## Jesus Says Catalog — AI Classification Ruleset

**Version:** 1.0  
**Applies to:** `teachings.json` — 31 categories, 125 subcategories, 700 teachings

---

## How to Use This Document

This ruleset governs every classification decision in the Jesus Says catalog. When assigning a teaching to a category and subcategory, follow this protocol:

1. **Read the teaching text and all its scripture references.** Identify the primary theological thrust — what the passage is *about*, not merely what it mentions.
2. **Check the candidate category's Theological Scope.** Does the primary thrust fall within it?
3. **Check the Explicit Exclusions.** If the teaching's primary thrust matches an exclusion, use the target category named there.
4. **Apply the Tie-Breaker Rule** when a teaching legitimately overlaps two categories. The tie-breaker is binding — it is not a suggestion.
5. **Assign to the most specific subcategory** whose description matches. If no subcategory fits, escalate for review rather than forcing a fit.
6. **Consult the Global Overrides** (below) before finalizing any assignment in categories 12, 27, 28, or 31. These categories have hard capture rules that override topical logic.

---

## Global Override Rules

These rules take unconditional precedence over all category-level logic:

| Rule | Override |
|---|---|
| **G-1: John 17** | ALL verses from John 17 belong to **Cat 12 (High Priestly Prayer)**. No John 17 verse belongs in any other category, regardless of topic. |
| **G-2: Passion Narrative** | ALL teachings spoken from the Last Supper through the Crucifixion (Matt 26–27 / Mark 14–15 / Luke 22–23 / John 13–19) belong to **Cat 27 (Passion Narrative)**. A teaching in this block that touches forgiveness, love, or prayer stays in Cat 27. |
| **G-3: Post-Resurrection** | ALL teachings spoken after the Resurrection (Matt 28 / Mark 16 / Luke 24 / John 20–21 / Acts resurrection appearances) belong to **Cat 28 (Post-Resurrection)**. Exception: the Great Commission is intentionally cross-listed in both Cat 26.5 and Cat 28.4. |
| **G-4: Revelation 1–3** | ALL direct speech of Christ in Rev 1–3 belongs to **Cat 31 (Seven Churches)**. No verse from Rev 1:10–3:22 belongs in any other category. |

## Category-Level Rules

---

## 1. God the Father

**Theological scope:** Teachings whose primary subject is God's character as Father — his care, his nature, and how he is to be known and worshipped. This category is about *who God is* as the Father, not about the mechanics of reaching him (that is salvation/prayer).

**Guiding principle:** The teaching illuminates the Father's identity, relational character, or manner of worship. If it is primarily about what a disciple *does* in response to God, it likely belongs in Discipleship, Prayer, or Righteousness.

**Inclusion themes:**
- God as Father, heavenly Father, Abba
- God's providential care for creation (sparrows, lilies)
- True versus false worship; worship in spirit and truth
- God's sovereignty, omniscience, love for humanity
- Seeking and finding the Father

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Praying *to* the Father → Cat 10 (Prayer and Communion)
- The Father sending the Son → Cat 2.2 (Relationship with the Father) when the focus is on Jesus's identity
- The Father's judgment → Cat 30 (Judgment and Hell)
- God's love expressed through sending Jesus → Cat 6 (Salvation) when the emphasis is the saving act

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching mentions both God's fatherly care and prayer, assign to Cat 1 if the point being made is *about God's nature* (he knows your needs, he is good). Assign to Cat 10 if the point is *instruction on how to pray*.

### Subcategories

#### 1.1 God's Fatherhood and Care
God as a caring, knowing, attentive Father who provides for his children. Distinguishable from 1.3 by its relational and pastoral register rather than its doctrinal weight.

#### 1.2 True Worship of God
Teachings about the manner and spirit of proper worship — what worship is, what it is not, and who God seeks as worshippers. Distinguishable from 14.4 (Authentic Piety), which is about the *practice* of giving/prayer/fasting, not the theology of worship itself.

#### 1.3 God's Sovereignty and Love
Teachings that assert God's supreme authority over creation, history, or human affairs, and his universal love. Distinguishable from 1.1 by its doctrinal rather than relational emphasis.

## 2. The Identity of Jesus Christ

**Theological scope:** Teachings in which Jesus makes explicit claims about his own nature, origin, divine authority, pre-existence, or messianic role. The subject of the teaching is *Jesus himself*.

**Guiding principle:** The test is whether Jesus is the *object* of the claim being made, not merely the speaker. If a teaching is instruction for disciples that happens to begin with "I am sending you," classify by the instruction's content, not by the "I am" opener.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "I AM" declarations (the bread, the light, the door, the resurrection, the way, the vine, the good shepherd)
- Oneness with the Father ("I and the Father are one")
- Claims of pre-existence ("Before Abraham was, I am")
- Divine authority to forgive sins, judge, raise the dead
- Predictions of his own death and resurrection
- Messianic fulfillment and the Son of Man title
- His origin from the Father; being sent by the Father

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Vine/branches teachings → Cat 11 (Abiding in Christ) because the emphasis is on the disciple's relationship, not on Jesus's identity alone
- "I am with you always" (Great Commission context) → Cat 28 (Post-Resurrection) per G-3
- Instructions to pray in his name → Cat 10.4 (Praying in His Name)
- The Father's greatness compared to the Son → classify by whether the passage is about Jesus's identity (Cat 2) or the Father's nature (Cat 1)

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching simultaneously reveals Jesus's identity and calls someone to faith or action, assign to Cat 2 if the dominant force is *disclosure* ("This is who I am"). Assign to Cat 6, 7, or 22 if the dominant force is *invitation or command* ("Come to me," "Follow me").

### Subcategories

#### 2.1 The "I AM" Declarations
The seven formal "I AM + predicate" statements in John and their parallels, plus extended teaching passages directly attached to those declarations (e.g., the John 10 shepherd discourse). Distinguishable from 2.2 by being metaphorical self-descriptions rather than relational assertions about the Father. Narrative passages that accompany an I AM context but are not themselves declarations (e.g., the Lazarus narrative, John 11) belong in 2.3.

#### 2.2 His Relationship with the Father
Teachings asserting unity, mutual knowledge, and mutual indwelling between Father and Son. Distinguishable from 2.1 by the explicitly relational rather than metaphorical framing.

#### 2.3 His Divine Authority and Mission
Claims of authority to forgive, judge, give life, and fulfill the Father's assignment. Distinguishable from 2.2 by focusing on what Jesus *does* rather than what he *is* in relation to the Father.

#### 2.4 His Pre-existence and Divine Origin
Teachings asserting existence before creation or before Abraham, and origin from above. Distinguishable from 2.2 by the temporal / cosmological rather than relational register.

#### 2.5 Predictions of His Suffering, Death, and Resurrection
Explicit passion predictions and statements about the Son of Man being delivered, killed, and raised. Note: teachings *during* the Passion events belong in Cat 27 per G-2. This subcategory holds the *advance predictions* spoken during ministry.

#### 2.6 His Messianic Identity
Teachings affirming fulfillment of Old Testament messianic expectation — the Christ, the Son of David, the one to come. Distinguishable from 2.3 by the covenantal/OT-fulfillment frame rather than a claim of present authority.

## 3. The Holy Spirit

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the person, work, and coming of the Holy Spirit. The Spirit must be the primary subject.

**Guiding principle:** Teachings about the Spirit's presence in the believer's life belong here. Teachings about praying in the Spirit belong in Cat 10. Teachings where the Spirit is mentioned only incidentally (e.g., "baptize in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit") may be cross-listed but classified by primary context.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Promise of the Paraclete / Advocate / Comforter
- The Spirit as guide into truth
- The Spirit's witness to and about Jesus
- The Spirit given to those who ask
- Conviction of sin, righteousness, judgment
- The Spirit speaking in persecution/trial
- Blasphemy against the Holy Spirit

**Explicit exclusions:**
- New birth language where "born of the Spirit" is in service of explaining salvation → Cat 6.1 (New Birth); if the teaching is primarily *about the Spirit's role* as agent of new birth, it may fit here
- Being filled/endued with the Spirit as preparation for mission → Cat 26 (Mission and Witness)

**Tie-breaker rule:** If a teaching mentions both the Spirit's work and salvation's condition, ask whether the point is "the Spirit does this" (Cat 3) or "you must do/believe this" (Cat 6/7).

### Subcategories

#### 3.1 The Promise and Coming of the Spirit
Forward-looking statements about the Spirit who will be sent. Predominantly from the Upper Room Discourse (John 14–16), pre-Passion context only; the Passion block belongs to Cat 27 per G-2.

#### 3.2 The Spirit's Work in the World
Teachings about what the Spirit accomplishes — conviction, revelation, glorification of Christ. Distinguishable from 3.1 by describing ongoing work rather than the event of coming.

#### 3.3 The Spirit in Witness and Trial
Teachings promising the Spirit will speak through disciples under persecution. Distinguishable from 3.2 by the persecution/courtroom setting.

#### 3.4 Blasphemy Against the Spirit
The one unforgivable sin teaching and its context. Stands alone; its legal/sin-classification content is not found in sibling subcategories.

## 4. The Kingdom of God

**Theological scope:** The Kingdom of God (or Kingdom of Heaven) as a theological concept — its nature, character, scope, value, growth, conditions of entry, and the characteristics of its citizens. This is the organizing center of Jesus's public teaching.

**Guiding principle:** The Kingdom must be the *subject* of the teaching, not merely the backdrop. "Enter the Kingdom" is a Kingdom teaching. "Follow me" with no Kingdom reference is Discipleship.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Kingdom of God / Kingdom of Heaven
- Inheriting the Kingdom; entering the Kingdom; being great in the Kingdom
- The nature of the Kingdom (not of this world; already/not yet; among you)
- Parables whose primary referent is the Kingdom itself
- The Beatitudes as portraits of Kingdom citizens
- Harvest imagery where the harvest IS the Kingdom

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Parables of lost things (sheep, coin, son) → Cat 5 (Repentance) because their primary thrust is the *value of one repentant sinner*, not the Kingdom's nature
- Parables of stewardship/money → Cat 20 (Wealth) unless money is only incidental to a Kingdom-value point
- Beatitudes/Sermon content about specific ethical behaviors → Cat 14 (Righteousness and Ethics)
- Kingdom entry in the context of repentance → Cat 5 if the emphasis is on turning; Cat 4.3 if the emphasis is on the Kingdom's conditions
- The Kingdom saying at the Last Supper → Cat 27 per G-2

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a parable references the Kingdom and repentance, the parable's punch line determines placement. If the climax is the finder's joy at recovery → Cat 5. If the climax is the Kingdom's surpassing value (sell all you have) → Cat 4.

### Subcategories

#### 4.1 Nature and Character of the Kingdom
Teachings that define what the Kingdom is — its qualities, source, and distinguishing features. Distinguishable from 4.2 by being definitional rather than developmental.

#### 4.2 Growth and Presence of the Kingdom
Parables and teachings about how the Kingdom expands and is already present (mustard seed, leaven, already among you). Distinguishable from 4.1 by the growth/presence dynamic.

#### 4.3 Entering the Kingdom
Teachings specifying conditions, gates, or barriers to entry. Distinguishable from Cat 6 (Salvation) by focusing on the *Kingdom threshold* rather than the salvation transaction; many overlap and should be cross-listed.

#### 4.4 Citizens of the Kingdom — The Beatitudes
The Beatitudes (Matt 5:3–12 / Luke 6:20–26) exclusively. This subcategory is reserved for the formal Beatitudes list.

#### 4.5 Parables of the Kingdom's People
Parables about how citizens of the Kingdom behave, relate, or are judged within it (wedding banquet behavior, talents, ten virgins). Distinguishable from 4.3 by depicting *life inside* the Kingdom rather than entry. Non-parable exchanges — such as authority challenges about the source of Jesus's power — belong in Cat 24.1.

## 5. Repentance and Conversion

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the call to turn from sin, the joy of restored relationship, and the invitation extended to those far from God. Emphasizes movement *toward* God.

**Guiding principle:** The emotional or theological center of gravity is the turning — the lost being found, the sinner being called. Not the Kingdom's inherent value, not the mechanics of salvation.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "Repent, for the kingdom is at hand"
- Lost sheep, lost coin, lost (prodigal) son
- Calling sinners, not the righteous
- The invitation to come and rest
- The narrow door in the context of the call to enter before it closes

**Explicit exclusions:**
- The salvation mechanism (faith, new birth) → Cat 6
- Conversion as the start of discipleship → Cat 22 (Discipleship) if the focus is the journey, not the turning point
- Kingdom parables whose climax is the Kingdom's value → Cat 4

**Tie-breaker rule:** A teaching about repentance told as a parable → Cat 5, NOT Cat 4, unless the primary meaning is the nature or value of the Kingdom itself (e.g., hidden treasure, pearl of great price → Cat 4).

### Subcategories

#### 5.1 The Call to Repentance
Direct commands and announcements to repent. Distinguishable from 5.2 by being propositional/declarative rather than narrative.

#### 5.2 Parables of the Lost
Lost sheep, lost coin, prodigal son, and close parallels. Each parable's climax is the joy of recovery.

#### 5.3 The Invitation to Come
Invitational teachings ("Come to me," "If anyone thirsts"). Distinguishable from 5.1 by the relational/emotional warmth of the invitation rather than the command register.

## 6. Salvation and Eternal Life

**Theological scope:** Teachings that describe the *transaction or outcome* of salvation — what it means to be saved, the role of faith as the instrument, the nature and assurance of eternal life, and the way that leads to it.

**Guiding principle:** The emphasis is on what God gives and how it is received. If the emphasis is on the *act of believing*, assign to Cat 7. If the emphasis is on the *life received*, assign here.

**Inclusion themes:**
- New birth (born again, born of water and Spirit)
- "For God so loved the world…" (saving purpose)
- Believe and have eternal life
- Eternal life defined (knowing God and Christ)
- The narrow gate and narrow way as entry to life
- "I give them eternal life, and they shall never perish"

**Explicit exclusions:**
- The act of believing under stress or doubt → Cat 7 (Faith and Trust)
- Entering the Kingdom → Cat 4.3 if Kingdom is the primary lens
- Forgiveness of specific sins → Cat 18 (Forgiveness) if the act of forgiving is the focus

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching describes both faith and eternal life, the decisive question is: what is the *promise being made*? If the promise is the outcome ("you will have eternal life") → Cat 6. If the teaching is instruction about how to believe or overcome unbelief → Cat 7.

### Subcategories

#### 6.1 The New Birth
John 3 and parallel new-birth teachings. The Spirit as agent of regeneration, water and Spirit, born from above.

#### 6.2 Faith as the Condition of Salvation
Teachings where faith is explicitly named as the means of receiving salvation or eternal life. Distinguishable from Cat 7 by anchoring faith in the *salvation outcome* rather than exploring its nature.

#### 6.3 Eternal Life — Its Nature and Assurance
Teachings describing what eternal life is, its security, and Jesus's role as its guarantor. Distinguishable from 6.2 by focusing on the gift rather than the condition.

#### 6.4 The Narrow Way
The narrow gate/road/way teachings, where the metaphor is explicitly about the path to life. Distinguishable from Cat 22.2 (Counting the Cost) by the eschatological life/death stakes, not just the cost of following.

## 7. Faith and Trust

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the nature, exercise, and power of faith itself — what faith is, how it works, how it overcomes fear, doubt, and anxiety. The *act and quality of believing* is the subject.

**Guiding principle:** Faith here is examined *as a phenomenon* — Jesus commending, rebuking, or teaching about faith. Not the object of faith (that belongs elsewhere) but the act of trusting.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "Your faith has made you well" / "O you of little faith"
- Moving mountains through faith
- Belief in Jesus as the antidote to troubled hearts
- "Do not be afraid; only believe"
- Overcoming worry and anxiety through trust in God
- The disciples' failure of faith in storms

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Faith as the condition of salvation → Cat 6.2 when the topic is *receiving* salvation
- Prayer requiring faith → Cat 10 when the topic is prayer practice
- Courage under persecution → Cat 23 (Suffering and Persecution)

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching addresses both faith and salvation, the primary emphasis determines placement. A teaching that says "believe in me and be saved" is primarily a salvation teaching (Cat 6.2). A teaching that rebukes "little faith" or demonstrates what faith accomplishes is primarily a faith teaching (Cat 7).

### Subcategories

#### 7.1 The Nature and Power of Faith
What faith is and what it can accomplish (mustard-seed faith, mountain-moving). Distinguishable from 7.2 by being constructive/descriptive rather than corrective.

#### 7.2 Overcoming Fear and Doubt
Teachings that address disciples' fear or doubt directly — rebuke or reassurance. Distinguishable from 7.3 by the situational crisis trigger (a storm, a death, Thomas's demand).

#### 7.3 Trust Over Anxiety
Teachings about releasing anxiety and worry through trust in God's provision. Distinguishable from 7.2 by the chronic/dispositional rather than crisis register (consider the lilies, do not be anxious).

## 8. The Old Covenant

**Theological scope:** Jesus's teaching about the Mosaic Law — its authority, its fulfillment, its limits, and the way the Hebrew Scriptures point forward to him.

**Guiding principle:** The Old Testament as a body of law and prophecy is the subject. If the teaching contrasts old and new but is primarily about the new covenant's institution (Lord's Supper), assign to Cat 9.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "I did not come to abolish the Law but to fulfill it"
- The Law's continued validity or modification
- Moses as witness to Jesus
- Scripture pointing to Jesus
- Tithing, temple duties, and their critique
- The Greatest Commandment (though cross-listed in Cat 13.1)

**Explicit exclusions:**
- The Antitheses ("You have heard… but I say") → Cat 14.3, because the primary subject is Jesus's ethical intensification, not a treatise on the Law itself
- Sabbath healings → Cat 24.4 (Sabbath in Right Perspective) when the frame is Pharisaical conflict
- The New Covenant meal → Cat 9 (New Covenant)

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching both cites the Law and announces new ethical teaching, assign to Cat 8 if the primary act is *quoting or affirming the Law*, and to Cat 14 if the primary act is *Jesus's new pronouncement*.

### Subcategories

#### 8.1 Jesus and the Law
Jesus's stance toward the Law's authority, validity, and scope. Includes the fulfillment statement and teachings about which commandments matter most. Note: the Temptation of Jesus (Matt 4:1–11 / Luke 4:1–13) belongs in Cat 23.1 — Jesus cites Scripture in that episode in the context of spiritual combat, not as a teaching on the Law itself.

#### 8.2 The Law's Fulfillment in Love
Teachings that summarize the Law under the two great commandments or as pointing to love. Closely related to Cat 13; assign here when the frame is explicitly the Law's summary, not the command to love as a standalone.

#### 8.3 Moses and the Scriptures Pointing to Jesus
Teachings where Jesus interprets OT texts or persons as prophetic witnesses to himself (Moses wrote of me, Abraham saw my day, the sign of Jonah).

## 9. The New Covenant

**Theological scope:** The establishment of the new covenant — the Lord's Supper as its institution, the Bread of Life discourse as theological preparation for it, and teachings about the transition from old to new.

**Guiding principle:** This category captures covenant *transition* and the eucharistic theology that supports it. Teachings about the ongoing experience of abiding in Christ belong in Cat 11.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "This is my body… this is my blood of the new covenant"
- The Bread of Life (John 6) as covenant-anticipating theology
- New wine / new wineskins (old vs. new era)
- The Lord's Supper in all synoptic parallels (pre-Passion context; see G-2 for Last Supper placement)

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Last Supper teachings *during* Passion week → Cat 27.1 per G-2
- Eucharist as part of discipleship teaching → Cat 22 if the frame is ongoing Christian practice (rare)
- Abiding in Christ as ongoing covenant relationship → Cat 11

**Tie-breaker rule:** John 6 bread-of-life sayings → Cat 9.2, not Cat 6 (Salvation), because their primary covenantal/sacramental content is the anchor, even though eternal life is mentioned.

### Subcategories

#### 9.1 Institution of the Lord's Supper
The institution words at the Last Supper across synoptic accounts. Note: per G-2, these teachings belong to Cat 27 when classified within the Passion Narrative context. This subcategory captures any pre-Passion teaching about the Supper's meaning.

#### 9.2 The Bread of Life — A Connected Teaching
John 6 discourse on eating the bread from heaven and drinking the blood. A theological antecedent to the Last Supper.

#### 9.3 New and Old — The Covenant Transition
Teachings about the incompatibility or transition between old and new (new wine/wineskins, new cloth on old garment). Primarily the metaphorical teachings marking the dawn of a new era.

## 10. Prayer and Communion

**Theological scope:** Jesus's teaching on prayer — its form, posture, persistence, power, and corporate practice. How to pray, not merely the fact that God hears.

**Guiding principle:** The teaching must be *instruction about prayer* or a *model for prayer*. Teachings where prayer is a narrative setting but the content is doctrinal go elsewhere.

**Inclusion themes:**
- The Lord's Prayer and its components
- Secret prayer versus public performance
- Persistence in asking (importunate widow, friend at midnight)
- Praying in Jesus's name and its efficacy
- Two or three gathered; corporate prayer
- Watchfulness in prayer; praying in Gethsemane (as instruction, not the Passion event itself)
- Asking, seeking, knocking

**Explicit exclusions:**
- The High Priestly Prayer (John 17) → Cat 12 per G-1
- Gethsemane prayer *as a Passion event* → Cat 27 per G-2
- Prayer as piety performance → Cat 14.4 (Authentic Piety) when the focus is exposing hypocrisy rather than teaching correct prayer
- Praying in the Spirit → if mentioned incidentally in a Spirit teaching, Cat 3 takes precedence

**Tie-breaker rule:** Teachings about praying in Jesus's name → Cat 10.4, NOT Cat 11 (Abiding), even though both draw from John 14–16. The topic of name-prayer is categorically a prayer instruction, not an abiding instruction.

### Subcategories

#### 10.1 The Lord's Prayer — Pattern for Prayer
The Lord's Prayer text and any immediate commentary on its petitions. Both Matthean and Lukan forms.

#### 10.2 How to Pray
Instructions on manner, location, disposition, and attitude in prayer (not in public for show; enter your closet; do not use vain repetitions). Distinguishable from 10.1 by being general instruction rather than a model text.

#### 10.3 Persistence in Prayer
Parables and teachings emphasizing continued asking and not losing heart. The widow and the judge; the friend at midnight.

#### 10.4 Praying in Jesus's Name
Teachings from the Upper Room Discourse about asking in Jesus's name and the Father's answer. Distinguishable from 11.2 (Abiding in Love) by the explicit name-prayer frame.

#### 10.5 Corporate Prayer and Watchfulness
"Where two or three are gathered" and Gethsemane's instruction to "watch and pray." Distinguishable from 10.2 by the communal or vigilance dimension.

## 11. Abiding in Christ

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the ongoing, mutual indwelling relationship between Christ and the disciple — primarily the Vine and Branches discourse and its immediate context in John 15.

**Guiding principle:** Abiding is the *relational and spiritual posture* of the disciple. It is distinct from prayer (Cat 10), love commands (Cat 13), and mission (Cat 26), even though all three appear in John 15.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Vine and branches (I am the vine; you are the branches)
- Abiding in my word; abiding in my love
- Bearing fruit as the result of abiding
- The mutual indwelling: you in me, I in you
- Remaining/continuing in Jesus as the condition of fruitfulness

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Praying in Jesus's name (John 15:16's "ask in my name") → Cat 10.4
- "Love one another" as the New Commandment (John 15:12) → Cat 13.4 (New Commandment)
- Mission to the world (John 15:18–27, the world's hatred and the Spirit's witness) → Cat 26 / Cat 3
- The "I AM the vine" declaration as a self-identification → Cat 2.1 (I AM Declarations) if the emphasis is the title; Cat 11 if the emphasis is the disciple's relationship to it

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching is from John 15 and involves multiple themes, classify by the *primary relational dynamic*: if it is about the disciple's union with Christ → Cat 11; if about love command → Cat 13; if about mission/persecution → Cat 26.

### Subcategories

#### 11.1 The Vine and the Branches
The core vine-and-branches metaphor (John 15:1–11) — fruitfulness, pruning, abiding, and the condition of bearing fruit. The vineyard imagery is the anchor.

#### 11.2 Abiding in Love
The transition from the vine metaphor to the love-and-obedience frame (John 15:9–17). The connecting logic is that abiding in love requires keeping Jesus's commands. Closely related to 13.4 but distinct in being the *mechanism* of abiding rather than the command itself.

## 12. The High Priestly Prayer

**Theological scope:** John 17 in its entirety — the only fully recorded spoken prayer of Jesus in the gospels, addressed to the Father on behalf of himself, his disciples, and all future believers.

**Guiding principle:** This category is captured exclusively by John 17. All verses from John 17 belong here and only here (Global Override G-1). There are no exclusions, because no other passages enter this category.

**Inclusion themes:**
- The hour of glorification
- Eternal life as knowing the Father and the Son
- Prayer for disciples' protection and sanctification
- Prayer for unity of all believers
- Jesus's shared glory with the Father before creation

### Subcategories

#### 12.1 Prayer for Himself — The Hour Has Come
John 17:1–5. Jesus prays for his own glorification and reviews the completion of his mission.

#### 12.2 Prayer for the Disciples
John 17:6–19. Intercession for the eleven — protection, truth, sanctification, sent-into-the-world.

#### 12.3 Prayer for All Believers
John 17:20–26. Intercession for all who will believe through the disciples' word — unity and shared glory.

---

## 13. Love

**Theological scope:** Teachings about love as a divine command and relational practice — love for God, neighbor, enemy, and fellow disciples.

**Guiding principle:** Love is the subject, not the backdrop. If love is mentioned in a broader ethical list, assign by the primary subject. The New Commandment belongs here even though it also appears in abiding contexts.

**Inclusion themes:**
- The two great commandments (love God, love neighbor)
- The New Commandment (love one another as I have loved you)
- Love of enemies, blessing those who curse
- The Good Samaritan as the definition of neighbor-love
- Self-sacrifice as the highest form of love ("greater love has no man")

**Explicit exclusions:**
- "Love your enemies" when the primary point is ethical nonretaliation → Cat 14.3 (Antitheses) if it appears in the Sermon context as an antithesis; Cat 13.3 if it stands as a standalone love teaching
- God's love for the world (as the basis of salvation) → Cat 6 when the point is the salvation act, Cat 1.3 when the point is God's character
- Abiding in love as the mechanism of union with Christ → Cat 11.2

**Tie-breaker rule:** "Love your enemies" appears both in the Sermon on the Mount Antitheses and as standalone teaching. When it occurs in the Antitheses block (Matt 5:43–48), classify under Cat 14.3. When it occurs independently or in Luke 6 outside the antithesis frame, classify under Cat 13.3.

### Subcategories

#### 13.1 Love for God
The first and greatest commandment. Often occurs alongside 13.2; assign both here when they appear as the paired summary of the Law.

#### 13.2 Love for Neighbor
The second great commandment and its illustration. The Good Samaritan parable belongs here.

#### 13.3 Love for Enemies
Teaching the radical extension of love beyond natural affection to opponents and persecutors. Outside the Antitheses block (see tie-breaker).

#### 13.4 The New Commandment
"Love one another as I have loved you" — the distinctively Johannine command given in the Upper Room context (pre-Passion ministry; note G-2 applies to Last Supper context).

---

## 14. Righteousness and Ethics

**Theological scope:** Jesus's moral teaching — the ethical demands of the Kingdom, covering character, speech, religious practice, discernment, and the foundational moral principle.

**Guiding principle:** This is the ethical "how to live" category. It covers both the Sermon on the Mount's ethical blocks and Jesus's standalone moral teachings.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Salt and light — the disciple's public moral witness
- The Law and Jesus's intensification of it
- Antitheses ("You have heard… but I say")
- Authentic piety: almsgiving, prayer, fasting done in secret
- Judging others; specks and planks
- The Golden Rule
- Two ways (broad and narrow roads) as an ethical call
- Oaths, truthfulness, and integrity of speech

**Explicit exclusions:**
- "You have heard…" blocks where love-of-enemies is the climax → Cat 13.3 if standalone; stays in Cat 14.3 if the full Antitheses block is the unit
- Sabbath teaching → Cat 24.4 when the Pharisaical conflict is the setting
- Truthfulness and oath-taking that is its own teaching unit → Cat 16.2
- The Golden Rule applied to prayer → Cat 10 if prayer is the primary context

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching appears within the Sermon on the Mount's ethical blocks, classify it by its subcategory within Cat 14 unless a Global Override applies. Individual sayings from the Sermon that are repeated elsewhere may be cross-listed.

### Subcategories

#### 14.1 Salt and Light
Matt 5:13–16. The disciple as moral/spiritual influence in the world.

#### 14.2 The Law and Righteousness
Matt 5:17–20. Jesus's affirmation of the Law's continuing validity and the demand for surpassing righteousness.

#### 14.3 The Antitheses — "You Have Heard… But I Say to You"
Matt 5:21–48. Six contrasts between traditional interpretation and Jesus's intensified demand. Includes murder/anger, adultery/lust, divorce, oaths, retaliation, love of enemies (within the antithesis block).

#### 14.4 Authentic Piety — Giving, Prayer, Fasting
Matt 6:1–18. Three-part teaching on acts of devotion done for God, not for human applause.

#### 14.5 Judging and Discernment
Matt 7:1–6 and parallels. Warnings against hypocritical judging; the speck and the plank; not casting pearls before swine. Distinguishable from Cat 17 (Wisdom) by the relational/ethical focus rather than the perceptual/spiritual focus.

#### 14.6 The Golden Rule and Two Ways
Matt 7:12–14 and parallels. The summary principle of moral action and the framing of two paths.

---

## 15. Humility and Servanthood

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the inversion of worldly status in the Kingdom — greatness through service, childlike posture, and the servant model Jesus himself embodied.

**Guiding principle:** The subject is the virtue of humility and the posture of service, often in contrast to worldly power or religious pride.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Greatness as servanthood ("whoever would be greatest among you")
- Jesus washing feet as the servant model
- The towel-and-basin ethic
- Children as models of Kingdom posture
- Parables of seating and rank (taking the lowest place)
- "Whoever exalts himself will be humbled"

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Hypocrisy of religious leaders seeking honor → Cat 24 (Religious Hypocrisy)
- The servant in parables of accountability → Cat 22 (Discipleship) if faithfulness, not humility, is the point
- Childlikeness as a condition of Kingdom entry → Cat 4.3 if the emphasis is entry; Cat 15.4 if the emphasis is the virtue itself

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching involves both humility and discipleship cost, assign to Cat 15 if the virtue being praised is servanthood/humility itself, and to Cat 22 if the point is the sacrifice required to follow.

### Subcategories

#### 15.1 The Nature of Greatness in the Kingdom
Teachings asserting that Kingdom greatness is defined by servanthood and lowliness.

#### 15.2 Jesus as the Servant Model
The foot-washing (John 13) and related teachings where Jesus himself is the paradigm of service.

#### 15.3 Parables of Humility
Narrative parables about posture, rank, and the reversal of social expectations (wedding seat parable, Pharisee and tax collector).

#### 15.4 Childlikeness as a Kingdom Virtue
Teachings where children or childlike posture are commended as Kingdom-appropriate.

---

## 16. Truth and Integrity

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the power and accountability of words, the demand for truthfulness, oaths, and Jesus's identification of himself as the Truth.

**Guiding principle:** The subject is integrity of speech and the relationship between truth and personhood. Teachings about truthfulness in prayer or witness belong in their respective categories.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Careless words and their judgment
- "Let your yes be yes and your no be no"
- Swearing oaths and their alternatives
- "I am the way, the truth, and the life" (as truth-identity teaching, though cross-listed in Cat 2.1)
- Words that defile from within
- Honesty versus performance religion

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Blasphemy against the Spirit → Cat 3.4
- Oaths in the Antitheses block → Cat 14.3 when it is part of the formal Antitheses list
- Witnessing truthfully under persecution → Cat 26 (Mission and Witness) or Cat 23

**Tie-breaker rule:** The oath/truthfulness teaching in Matt 5:33–37 (one of the Antitheses) classifies under Cat 14.3 when treated as part of the Antitheses block, and under Cat 16.2 when treated as a standalone teaching on truthfulness.

### Subcategories

#### 16.1 The Power and Accountability of Words
Teachings on the weight of spoken words — careless speech, words from the overflow of the heart, accountability for every idle word.

#### 16.2 Truthfulness and Oaths
The demand for plain yes/no speech and the critique of oath systems. Related to Cat 14.3 (see tie-breaker).

#### 16.3 Jesus as Truth
The "I am the truth" declaration and associated teachings on Jesus as the source and standard of truth. Often cross-listed with Cat 2.1.

---

## 17. Wisdom and Discernment

**Theological scope:** Teachings about spiritual perception — the ability to hear, see, understand, recognize truth, and distinguish false from genuine. The cognitive and perceptual dimensions of spiritual life.

**Guiding principle:** The subject is *how one perceives* spiritual reality. If the teaching is about what one *does* with perception (judging others ethically), assign to Cat 14.5. If about how one *gains* perception, assign here.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "He who has ears to hear, let him hear"
- The parable of the sower explained (types of hearers)
- Parables as a means of concealing and revealing
- Spiritual blindness rebuked in Pharisees
- Recognizing false prophets by their fruit
- Lamp and eye sayings (the eye as the lamp of the body)
- Hidden things revealed to the childlike

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Judging others as an ethical practice → Cat 14.5
- Recognizing the signs of the end times → Cat 29 (Eschatology)
- The Pharisees' blindness in conflict over healing → Cat 24 (Religious Hypocrisy) if the primary charge is hypocrisy

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching rebukes both blindness and hypocrisy, ask whether the rebuke is about *failing to perceive* (Cat 17) or *pretending to perceive while acting falsely* (Cat 24). Willful hypocrisy → Cat 24. Genuine perceptual failure → Cat 17.

### Subcategories

#### 17.1 Hearing and Perceiving Spiritually
Teachings about the spiritual ear — the call to hear, the parable of the sower as a hearing parable, the mystery given to disciples.

#### 17.2 Spiritual Blindness Rebuked
Teachings that rebuke leaders or crowds for failure of spiritual perception. Distinguishable from Cat 24 by the perceptual rather than moral/behavioral diagnosis.

#### 17.3 Recognizing True from False
Teachings about discerning true prophets/teachers from false ones (fruit test, wolves in sheep's clothing).

#### 17.4 Hidden Things Revealed
Teachings about revelation of what was hidden — to the humble, to disciples, through the Spirit. Parables explained; mysteries of the Kingdom disclosed.

---

## 18. Forgiveness and Reconciliation

**Theological scope:** God's act of forgiving sins and the disciple's obligation to forgive others and pursue reconciliation.

**Guiding principle:** The theological act of forgiveness — divine or human — is the primary subject. Mercy as a general disposition belongs partly here and partly in Cat 21.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "Your sins are forgiven"
- The parable of the unforgiving servant
- "Forgive us our debts as we forgive our debtors"
- "If your brother sins, rebuke him; if he repents, forgive him"
- Reconciliation before altar-offering
- The seventy-times-seven rule of forgiveness

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Forgiveness from the cross ("Father, forgive them") → Cat 27 per G-2
- Mercy as social justice (caring for the poor) → Cat 21 (Justice and Mercy)
- Repentance as the precondition of forgiveness → Cat 5 if the turning is primary
- Binding and loosing → Cat 25.2 (Church Discipline) when it is about church authority

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching involves both forgiveness and repentance, ask whether the *act of forgiving* or the *act of turning* is the punch line. "Go and sin no more" → the turn is primary → Cat 5. "Your sins are forgiven" → the grant is primary → Cat 18.1.

### Subcategories

#### 18.1 God's Forgiveness of Sin
Teachings where Jesus pronounces or promises divine forgiveness. Often tied to healing narratives — classify here when the forgiveness pronouncement is the theologically prominent act.

#### 18.2 Forgiving Others
The obligation and limitlessness of human forgiveness. The unforgiving servant parable belongs here.

#### 18.3 Reconciliation with Others
Teachings about restoring broken human relationships (Matt 5:23–24, Matt 18:15–17). Closely related to 18.2 but focused on the relational restoration process, not just the act of pardoning.

---

## 19. Marriage and Family

**Theological scope:** Jesus's teaching on the institutions of marriage, family, and human sexuality, and the Kingdom's claim over biological family loyalties.

**Guiding principle:** The subject is the created and covenant institution of marriage/family. Teachings about sex as ethical command belong here. Teachings about forsaking family for Kingdom belong in Cat 22 (Discipleship) when the call to follow is the primary point.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Marriage as God's design from creation
- Divorce and its limits
- Lust and sexual purity
- Celibacy for the Kingdom
- Children and their value/protection
- Kingdom priority over family ties ("I came to set a man against his father")
- Honoring parents as Law commandment in Jesus's teaching

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Forsaking family to follow Jesus → Cat 22 (Discipleship) when the frame is the cost of following, not the theology of family itself
- Widows and economic vulnerability → Cat 21 (Justice and Mercy) when justice is the frame
- The wedding banquet as a Kingdom parable → Cat 4.5

**Tie-breaker rule:** "I came not to bring peace but a sword" (Matt 10:34–36, dividing family members) → Cat 23.2 (Promise of Persecution) if the emphasis is the division caused by witness; Cat 19.3 if the emphasis is the Kingdom's claim on family loyalties. Context of the passage determines the assignment.

### Subcategories

#### 19.1 Marriage and Divorce
Teachings on the permanence of marriage, grounds for divorce, and remarriage. All synoptic divorce texts.

#### 19.2 Sexual Purity
Teachings on lust, adultery of the heart, and celibacy for the Kingdom.

#### 19.3 Family Relationships and Kingdom Priority
Teachings on the claim the Kingdom makes over family ties — honoring parents, family division, true family as those who do God's will.

#### 19.4 Care for Children
Teachings about protecting, welcoming, and not causing children to stumble.

---

## 20. Wealth and Generosity

**Theological scope:** Jesus's teaching on money, possessions, material goods — the danger of wealth, the call to generosity, and the principle of right relationship to material things.

**Guiding principle:** Money or material possessions must be the *primary subject*. If money appears in a parable only as the vehicle for a different lesson (e.g., forgiveness, kingdom entry), classify by the primary lesson.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "You cannot serve God and money (mammon)"
- The rich young ruler
- Zacchaeus
- The parable of the rich fool
- The rich man and Lazarus
- Treasure in heaven versus earth
- Giving to the poor, to Caesar, to God
- The widow's offering
- Stewardship parables where the goods/talents are literal wealth

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Parables of talents/minas where the theological weight is about using gifts for the Kingdom → Cat 22 (Discipleship) or Cat 29 (Eschatology); assign to Cat 20 only if the focus is on the *material stewardship itself*
- The question of paying taxes (render to Caesar) → may fit here or Cat 8 (Old Covenant) if the question is about the Law; classify by primary issue
- The Beatitudes about the poor → Cat 4.4 (Beatitudes)

**Tie-breaker rule:** A parable about stewardship of money → Cat 20, NOT Cat 22 (Discipleship), unless money is only incidental to a Kingdom-faithfulness point. The talents parable, depending on emphasis: if the master's return and accountability is the frame, Cat 29 or Cat 22; if the investment of literal resources is the frame, Cat 20.

### Subcategories

#### 20.1 Treasure and the Heart
Teachings on where one stores treasure and what that reveals about the heart. The two-master teaching.

#### 20.2 The Danger of Riches
Teachings warning of wealth's spiritual peril — the rich young ruler, camel through the eye of a needle, rich fool.

#### 20.3 Generosity and Stewardship
Teachings calling for active generosity and faithful use of resources — giving to Caesar and God, the widow's mite.

#### 20.4 The Rich Man and Lazarus
The parable of Lazarus and the rich man (Luke 16:19–31). Stands alone in this subcategory due to its eschatological and social justice dimensions.

---

## 21. Justice and Mercy

**Theological scope:** Teachings about God's concern for the poor, the oppressed, and the vulnerable, and the final judgment predicated on how one treated them. Social righteousness as a dimension of the Kingdom.

**Guiding principle:** The theological subject is *social and economic justice* — care for the marginalized as a Kingdom demand. Distinguish from Cat 18 (Forgiveness) by the social/structural rather than relational/personal frame.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Woes to the rich; blessings on the poor (Luke 6)
- The Sheep and Goats judgment (Matt 25)
- Caring for "the least of these"
- Healing as mercy
- The Good Samaritan (though cross-listed in Cat 13.2, assign here if the mercy-to-the-vulnerable is the primary frame)
- Woe to those who exploit widows

**Explicit exclusions:**
- The rich man and Lazarus → Cat 20.4, because the primary structural context is wealth/poverty rather than judgment of mercy
- Forgiveness of personal wrongs → Cat 18
- Beatitudes blessings on the poor → Cat 4.4 (Beatitudes)

**Tie-breaker rule:** The Sheep and Goats (Matt 25) stays in Cat 21.2 and is not assigned to Cat 30 (Judgment), because the primary theological load is the criterion of mercy, not the mechanics of judgment.

### Subcategories

#### 21.1 The Blessing on the Poor and Woe to the Rich
Luke 6:20–26 woes and blessings in their social-justice dimension. Distinguishable from Cat 4.4 (Beatitudes) by treating the Lukan form's explicit social contrast.

#### 21.2 The Judgment of the Nations — Sheep and Goats
Matt 25:31–46. The criterion of mercy toward the vulnerable as the mark of genuine discipleship. The eschatological frame serves the mercy teaching.

#### 21.3 Mercy and Compassion
Teachings calling for, modeling, or commending mercy and compassion in direct speech ("Go and learn what this means: I desire mercy and not sacrifice").

---

## 22. Discipleship

**Theological scope:** Teachings about what it means and costs to follow Jesus — the call, the demands, the fruit, the endurance, and the commission to make more disciples.

**Guiding principle:** Following Jesus as the *defining life posture* is the subject. Teachings about specific virtues of the disciple belong in their respective categories (humility, love, etc.) unless the context is the call to follow.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "Follow me" calls
- Counting the cost of discipleship
- Taking up the cross daily
- Losing life to find it
- Bearing fruit as evidence of discipleship
- Remaining faithful to the end
- Making and teaching disciples

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Specific virtues of discipleship life → their own categories (humility → Cat 15; love → Cat 13; prayer → Cat 10)
- Suffering for Jesus → Cat 23 when persecution is the primary frame
- The servant parables about accountability at the master's return → Cat 29 if end-times judgment is primary; Cat 22 if the practical faithfulness of serving is primary

**Tie-breaker rule:** Healing narratives where Jesus says "follow me" after healing → Cat 22.1 if the call-to-follow is the climax; Cat 2.3 if the healing-authority claim is the climax.

### Subcategories

#### 22.1 The Call to Follow
Direct calls — "Follow me," "Come after me." The initial summons of discipleship.

#### 22.2 Counting the Cost
Teachings on the demanding, potentially costly nature of following Jesus. Building the tower; going to war; selling all.

#### 22.3 Fruit-Bearing
Teachings on the expectation and evidence of fruit in the disciple's life. The fruitless fig tree; "by their fruits you will know them."

#### 22.4 Remaining Faithful to the End
Teachings on perseverance — endurance through opposition, not looking back, the servant who remains at his post.

---

## 23. Suffering and Persecution

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the inevitability of persecution for following Jesus, courage in the face of suffering, and the meaning of cross-bearing.

**Guiding principle:** The frame is suffering *specifically caused by Christian witness and allegiance to Jesus*. General suffering from life circumstances is not the subject.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "You will be hated by all nations on account of my name"
- Fear of those who can kill the body, not the soul
- The Spirit speaking when persecuted
- Cross-bearing as the cost of following
- "He who loses his life will save it"
- Being dragged before governors and kings

**Explicit exclusions:**
- The Spirit speaking in trial → Cat 3.3 if the Spirit is the primary subject
- Persecution listed among end-times signs → Cat 29 when the eschatological context is primary
- The Beatitude on the persecuted → Cat 4.4 (Beatitudes)

**Tie-breaker rule:** "Take up your cross" sayings → Cat 23.4 if the context is literal suffering and cost of witness; Cat 22.2 if the context is the general cost of discipleship without persecution framing.

### Subcategories

#### 23.1 The Temptation of Jesus
Jesus's responses to Satan's three temptations (Matt 4:1–11 / Luke 4:1–13). Each response is a citation of Deuteronomy wielded in spiritual combat. Distinguishable from Cat 8.1 by being adversarial confrontation, not instruction on the Law.

#### 23.2 The Promise of Persecution
Teachings stating that persecution will come and why. Prophetic and preparatory in character.

#### 23.3 Courage in Suffering
Teachings calling for courage, fearlessness, and bold witness under threat.

#### 23.4 Taking Up the Cross
Cross-bearing teachings — denying self, taking up the cross, losing one's life for Christ.

---

## 24. Religious Hypocrisy

**Theological scope:** Jesus's sustained critique of false religion — the gap between outward observance and inward reality, the corruption of religious leadership, and the misuse of Sabbath and tradition.

**Guiding principle:** The primary subject is religious falsehood and its exposure. Ethical teaching addressed to disciples (not to expose Pharisees) belongs in Cat 14.

**Inclusion themes:**
- The Seven Woes of Matthew 23
- "Whitewashed tombs" and external cleansing
- Tradition of men nullifying God's word (Corban)
- Sabbath healing controversies (Jesus's responses to accusation)
- Blind guides leading the blind
- Hypocrisy of religious leaders seeking recognition

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Authentic piety as positive instruction to disciples → Cat 14.4
- Spiritual blindness as a perceptual failure → Cat 17.2 (though many overlap; assign to Cat 24 if hypocrisy/pretense is the charge, Cat 17 if genuine blindness is the charge)
- Love of money critiqued in Pharisees → Cat 20 if the primary subject is wealth

**Tie-breaker rule:** When rebuking both blindness and hypocrisy: willful pretense of righteousness → Cat 24. Genuine failure to perceive → Cat 17. Sabbath healings are Cat 24.4 when the Pharisaical accusation is the occasion; they are Cat 2.3 when Jesus's authority is the point being made.

### Subcategories

#### 24.1 The Seven Woes to Scribes and Pharisees (Matthew 23)
Matt 23:13–36. The formal woe-series. Each woe should be assigned here.

#### 24.2 Outward Versus Inward Religion
Teachings contrasting external observance with internal transformation — what defiles a person, clean outside/dirty inside.

#### 24.3 Traditions of Men Versus God's Commands
The Corban controversy and similar teachings about human tradition overriding divine command.

#### 24.4 The Sabbath in Right Perspective
Jesus's teachings during Sabbath controversies — healing on the Sabbath, the Sabbath made for man.

#### 24.5 Hypocrisy in Leadership
Teachings on leaders who seek prestige, exploit the vulnerable, or fail to do what they teach.

---

## 25. The Church

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the ekklesia — the community of believers, its foundation, authority, discipline, and corporate life.

**Guiding principle:** The *institutional* or *communal* dimension of the people of God is the subject. Individual discipleship belongs in Cat 22; corporate prayer in Cat 10.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "On this rock I will build my church"
- Keys of the Kingdom (binding and loosing)
- Church discipline process (Matt 18:15–20)
- "Where two or three are gathered in my name"
- Unity as a distinguishing mark of the community
- The washing of feet as community ethic (cross-listed with Cat 15.2)

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Corporate prayer → Cat 10.5 if the focus is prayer practice
- The Great Commission → Cat 26.5 / Cat 28.4
- Instructions to the Twelve on mission → Cat 26 (Mission and Witness)
- Unity prayed for in John 17 → Cat 12.3

**Tie-breaker rule:** "Where two or three are gathered" → Cat 10.5 if the teaching is primarily about the power of corporate prayer; Cat 25.3 if the teaching is primarily about Christ's presence in the gathered community.

### Subcategories

#### 25.1 The Foundation of the Church
The Peter/rock saying and the promise that the gates of hell will not prevail.

#### 25.2 Church Discipline
The binding and loosing authority and the Matthew 18 process for addressing a sinning brother.

#### 25.3 Corporate Prayer and Presence
"Where two or three are gathered" and the promise of Christ's presence in the community.

#### 25.4 Unity Among Believers
Teachings explicitly calling for or describing the unity of believers as a community.

---

## 26. Mission and Witness

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the active outward movement of the gospel — the harvest, the sending of workers, the instructions for mission, and courage in witness.

**Guiding principle:** The movement is *outward* — disciples sent into the world. Teachings about the disciple's inward formation belong elsewhere. The Spirit's enabling of witness is shared with Cat 3.3.

**Inclusion themes:**
- "The harvest is plentiful, but the laborers are few"
- Instructions to the Twelve (Matt 10) and the Seventy (Luke 10)
- "Go and make disciples of all nations" (Great Commission; also cross-listed in Cat 28.4)
- Shaking the dust off feet
- Being wise as serpents and harmless as doves
- Visions and commissions in Acts (Paul on the road to Damascus, Peter's Cornelius vision)
- "You will be my witnesses to the ends of the earth"

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Courage under persecution (with no mission context) → Cat 23
- The Spirit speaking in court → Cat 3.3
- Post-resurrection appearances' commission → Cat 28 per G-3, with the exception of the Great Commission, which is intentionally held in both Cat 26.5 and Cat 28.4

**Tie-breaker rule:** Instructions that occur in the Passion narrative context → Cat 27 per G-2, even if they include mission elements. Instructions given in ministry or post-resurrection → classify normally.

### Subcategories

#### 26.1 The Harvest and the Workers
The harvest metaphor and its call to prayer for workers and active participation in the mission.

#### 26.2 Instructions for the Twelve and the Seventy
Practical and theological instructions for sent workers — what to take, how to greet, what to do with reception and rejection.

#### 26.3 Fear and Courage in Witness
Teachings calling missionaries and witnesses to fearlessness.

#### 26.4 Persecution of Witnesses
Teachings describing the hostile reception witnesses will face — specifically in the context of active witness and proclamation.

#### 26.5 The Great Commission
The post-resurrection mandate to make disciples of all nations (Matt 28:18–20; John 20:21–23; Acts 1:8 and parallels). Cross-listed in Cat 28.4.

#### 26.6 Visions and Commissions to the Apostles
Post-ascension appearances and commissions recorded in Acts — the road to Damascus, the Cornelius vision, and similar — where the risen Christ speaks directly.

---

## 27. The Passion Narrative

**Theological scope:** ALL teachings spoken by Jesus from the Last Supper through the Crucifixion and his death. Temporal boundaries: from Jesus's words at the Passover meal (Matt 26:20 / Mark 14:17 / Luke 22:14 / John 13:1) through "It is finished" and the final cry from the cross.

**Guiding principle:** This category is governed by **Global Override G-2**. Temporal context, not topic, determines classification. A teaching in this block that touches forgiveness, prayer, love, or the Spirit stays in Cat 27. No teaching in this window is re-assigned to another category based on topic.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Institution of the Lord's Supper
- Foot washing and servant leadership (John 13)
- Upper Room Discourse (John 13–16, minus John 17 which is Cat 12)
- Prediction of Peter's denial
- Gethsemane — the prayer and the arrest dialogue
- Statements at trial (before Pilate, before Caiaphas)
- The Seven Last Words from the Cross

**Note on Upper Room Discourse:** John 13–16 falls within the G-2 window. All teachings in these chapters (except John 17 which is G-1) belong in Cat 27, even though many would topically fit Cat 3 (Spirit), Cat 10 (Prayer), Cat 11 (Abiding), or Cat 13 (Love).

### Subcategories

#### 27.1 The Last Supper
Words spoken during the Passover meal — institution of the Lord's Supper, foot washing, the Upper Room Discourse through John 16.

#### 27.2 Gethsemane and Arrest
Gethsemane prayer (as narrative event, not instruction), the arrest dialogue.

#### 27.3 The Trial and Crucifixion
Words spoken before Caiaphas, Pilate, and Herod; words spoken en route to Golgotha.

#### 27.4 Words from the Cross (the seven last words)
The seven sayings from the cross: Father forgive them; today in paradise; woman behold your son; My God why have you forsaken me; I thirst; it is finished; Father into your hands.

---

## 28. Post-Resurrection Appearances

**Theological scope:** ALL teachings spoken by Jesus after the Resurrection (per **Global Override G-3**). Temporal boundary begins at the empty tomb morning and extends through the Ascension.

**Guiding principle:** Temporal context, not topic, determines classification. Post-resurrection words are assigned here. The Great Commission is intentionally cross-listed in Cat 26.5 because it also functions as the culminating mission sending.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Words to Mary Magdalene and the women
- Words to disciples on Emmaus road
- Words to gathered disciples ("Peace be with you," show of wounds)
- "Do not be afraid; I am the first and the last" (Rev 1 context is Cat 31)
- Words to Thomas
- Words to Peter by the sea (restoration and commission)
- The Great Commission (Matt 28:18–20; Mark 16:15–18; Luke 24:46–49; Acts 1:4–8)

### Subcategories

#### 28.1 Resurrection Appearances — Words Spoken
General resurrection appearance words — to the women, on the road to Emmaus, to the gathered disciples. All post-resurrection speech not covered by a more specific subcategory.

#### 28.2 Thomas — Doubt Confronted by Evidence
John 20:26–29. The specific Thomas encounter.

#### 28.3 The Restoration of Peter
John 21:15–23. The threefold restoration and commission of Peter.

#### 28.4 The Final Commission and Ascension
The Great Commission in all its forms and the words spoken at the Ascension. Cross-listed with Cat 26.5.

---

## 29. Eschatology and the End Times

**Theological scope:** Jesus's teaching about the end of the age — signs preceding it, the return of the Son of Man, the imperative of readiness, and the parabolic teaching about watchfulness.

**Guiding principle:** The frame is the *final eschaton* — not personal death and afterlife (Cat 30), but cosmic and historical ending. Parables of readiness belong here when the master's return is explicitly the frame.

**Inclusion themes:**
- The Olivet Discourse (Matt 24 / Mark 13 / Luke 21)
- Signs of the end (wars, earthquakes, false Christs)
- The abomination of desolation
- The return of the Son of Man in glory
- The Fig Tree parable (season recognition)
- "No one knows the day or hour"
- Parables of readiness: ten virgins, faithful/unfaithful servant, talents (in their eschatological frame)

**Explicit exclusions:**
- Hell and final judgment → Cat 30 when the primary subject is judgment/condemnation
- Sheep and Goats (though eschatological) → Cat 21.2 because the mercy-criterion is primary
- Persecution of disciples as a sign → Cat 23 when the personal experience is primary; may be cross-listed when part of the Olivet Discourse

**Tie-breaker rule:** Olivet Discourse teachings → Cat 29 unless a Global Override applies. Parables of accountability at a master's return → Cat 29 if the eschatological return is the explicit frame; Cat 22 if the master's return is only a literary device for a faithfulness teaching.

### Subcategories

#### 29.1 Signs of the End
The catalog of signs — wars, famines, false prophets, persecution, the abomination of desolation. Primarily the Olivet Discourse's first half.

#### 29.2 The Return of the Son of Man
Teachings about the manner and glory of the Parousia — coming on clouds, gathering the elect.

#### 29.3 Readiness and Watchfulness
Parables and teachings calling for active, sustained readiness — the ten virgins, the thief in the night, the watching servants.

#### 29.4 The Fig Tree and the Unknown Hour
The Fig Tree as season-sign; the unknowability of the exact hour; the Parousia as sovereign and sudden.

---

## 30. Judgment and Hell

**Theological scope:** Teachings about the reality and certainty of final judgment, the nature of hell/Gehenna, and the resurrection's connection to final destiny.

**Guiding principle:** The primary subject is God's judicial act of evaluating souls and the eternal consequence of rejection. Distinguishable from Cat 29 (Eschatology) by focusing on the *personal, judicial outcome* rather than the cosmic/historical signs.

**Inclusion themes:**
- Gehenna / hell — "where the worm does not die and the fire is not quenched"
- Weeping and gnashing of teeth
- The outer darkness
- "Do not fear him who can kill the body, but fear him who can destroy both body and soul in hell"
- The resurrection of the just and unjust
- "Depart from me, I never knew you"
- Judgment on Capernaum, Chorazin, Bethsaida

**Explicit exclusions:**
- The Sheep and Goats → Cat 21.2 (mercy-criterion is primary)
- The Rich Man and Lazarus → Cat 20.4 (wealth framing is primary)
- Olivet Discourse signs → Cat 29
- Judgment of the nations as a sign of the end → Cat 29

**Tie-breaker rule:** When a teaching both describes hell's nature and calls for readiness, assign to Cat 30 if the hell description is the dominant image. Assign to Cat 29 if readiness/watchfulness is the dominant call with hell as the implied consequence.

### Subcategories

#### 30.1 The Certainty and Scope of Judgment
Teachings asserting the certainty of divine judgment and its universal scope. "Every idle word… will be judged"; judgment on the cities.

#### 30.2 Hell and Its Reality
Teachings describing Gehenna, eternal fire, outer darkness — the nature of final condemnation. Jesus's most vivid descriptions of hell belong here.

#### 30.3 The Resurrection and Final Destiny
Teachings on bodily resurrection (denial of the Sadducees; "in my Father's house are many rooms"; the resurrection of life and of condemnation in John 5).

## 31. The Seven Churches

**Theological scope:** ALL direct speech of Christ in Revelation 1–3, governed by **Global Override G-4**. The glorified Christ's messages to seven first-century churches and the framing vision of Revelation 1.

**Guiding principle:** No classification logic is needed — Global Override G-4 captures every verse of Rev 1:10–3:22. The subcategory structure follows the text's own seven-letter outline.

**Inclusion themes:**
- The vision of the Son of Man among the lampstands (Rev 1)
- The sevenfold "I know your works" address structure
- Individual church assessments: commendation, rebuke, warning, promise
- The call to "hear what the Spirit says to the churches"
- The promise to the overcomer (each letter)

### Subcategories

#### 31.1 Introduction — The Vision of Christ (Rev 1:11–20)
The inaugural vision — the Son of Man among the lampstands, his appearance, and his self-identification ("I am the first and the last, the living one").

#### 31.2 Ephesus — The Church That Left Its First Love
Rev 2:1–7. Commended for toil and doctrinal faithfulness; rebuked for abandoning first love.

#### 31.3 Smyrna — The Suffering Church
Rev 2:8–11. No rebuke; encouraged through imminent suffering and the promise of the crown of life.

#### 31.4 Pergamum — The Compromised Church
Rev 2:12–17. Commended for not denying faith; rebuked for tolerating Balaam-like teaching and the Nicolaitans.

#### 31.5 Thyatira — The Church Tolerating False Teaching
Rev 2:18–29. The longest letter; rebuked for tolerating the prophetess "Jezebel" and her sexual immorality.

#### 31.6 Sardis — The Dead Church
Rev 3:1–6. Rebuked for having a reputation for life while being spiritually dead; call to wake up.

#### 31.7 Philadelphia — The Faithful Church
Rev 3:7–13. No rebuke; commended for keeping the word with little strength; the open door promise.

#### 31.8 Laodicea — The Lukewarm Church
Rev 3:14–22. Rebuked for lukewarmness; the famous invitation: "I stand at the door and knock."
